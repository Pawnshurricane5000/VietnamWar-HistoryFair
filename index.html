Drafted Into Dissent

How public resistance to the Vietnam War draft exposed the inequalities of conscription and ended seven decades of compulsory American military service.

Era 1964 – 1973
Category Individual Website
Authors Atrey Iyer · Hemanth Samayamantri · Anuj Subramanian

A lottery promising fairness through randomness became, in practice, a system that exempted the wealthy and consumed the poor. When Americans saw it for what it was, they did not just protest the war. They refused to be drafted into it.

Thesis Statement

Public resistance against the Vietnam War draft transformed American conscription to a great extent, contributing decisively to the shift toward an all-volunteer force. Widespread civil disobedience made the draft impossible to enforce, while exposed racial and class inequalities drew Civil Rights organizations into the fight and turned the lottery into a national reckoning.

Research Question

To what extent did public resistance to the Vietnam War draft, particularly opposition to student, class-based, and racial exemptions, transform America's system of military conscription and contribute to the shift toward an all-volunteer force?

Why this topic matters

The Vietnam War draft is one of the most consequential reforms in modern American history. It ended one system, compulsory military service, and produced another, the all-volunteer force, that has defined every American war since. But the story behind that reform is not a story of orderly policymaking. It is a story of refusal.

Roughly 2.5 million Americans were drafted between 1960 and 1973. Hundreds of thousands deserted; more than a million evaded the draft in some form. The system that survived two world wars collapsed in a decade, not because political leaders chose to end it, but because the public chose to stop complying with it.
