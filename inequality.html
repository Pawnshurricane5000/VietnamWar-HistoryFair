Sub-Claim 2

The Lottery Was Never Random

Class and racial inequality at the heart of the draft

Beneath the rhetoric of fairness, the draft systematically protected the wealthy and educated while consuming working-class and minority men. The lottery that was supposed to spread the burden of service equally became, in practice, one of the most visible engines of class and racial inequality in postwar America. Once the public came to understand this clearly, the moral case for compulsory service collapsed.

The legal architecture of escape

The Selective Service system was not a single instrument. It was a set of overlapping rules whose effect depended heavily on who you were and what you could afford. The brightest registrants were often channeled into safer assignments like the reserves, while those with less education and fewer resources were sent disproportionately into combat (McCann, "Draft Evasion"). Educational deferments, in particular, became the most consequential class divider of the era: a man enrolled in college could legally postpone induction, sometimes for years. A man working full-time at eighteen could not.

Even when the lottery was nominally random, the system surrounding it was not. Hiring a private attorney, the kind of resource available only to wealthier families, often produced a medical or hardship deferment regardless of underlying condition. In a 1988 Los Angeles Times account, an anonymous draft dodger described doing exactly that, explaining without apology that he had paid a lawyer to keep him out, and that he felt no guilt about whoever had been sent in his place. He argued that responsibility for the imbalance rested with the government that had built the system, not with the men wealthy enough to escape it ("Vietnam War Draft Dodgers," Los Angeles Times).

Channeling the bright, drafting the rest

As the war intensified, the federal government tightened deferment categories. The 1969 lottery formalized the selection process, but statisticians quickly demonstrated that the drawing was not truly random; later months of the year were systematically overrepresented in the early picks (Caffrey, "Draft Lottery (1969)"). Despite legal challenges, the courts upheld the lottery. The randomness was, in the strict mathematical sense, a fiction. The deeper inequities, who could afford lawyers, who could enroll in college, who would draw the long capsule, all stacked the burden of combat on the men least equipped to escape it.

The pattern extended beyond the lottery itself. Once inducted, soldiers were not assigned to combat roles at random either. Those with more education and better-connected families were more likely to land in clerical, reserve, or stateside positions, while those without those advantages were funneled toward the front. The result was a Vietnam-era military whose combat ranks looked dramatically different from the country that supplied it, a fact that would not stay quiet for long once journalists, antiwar organizers, and Civil Rights leaders began to publish the numbers.

Naming the injustice

The most direct public statement of these inequalities came in a widely circulated 1967 antidraft petition. The document argued the war was undeclared by Congress and therefore unconstitutional, that it violated the moral and religious beliefs of millions of Americans, and that it had drafted African Americans in disproportionate numbers (Accettola, "Civil Rights, Social Justice, and Antiwar Activism"). Comparing U.S. conduct in Vietnam to the crimes prosecuted at Nuremberg, the petition framed resistance not as lawbreaking but as moral duty under the First Amendment.

The significance of the petition was not the document itself but the alliance it represented. Class-based criticism of the draft had existed since the Civil War. What was new in the 1960s was the fusion of that criticism with the moral language of the Civil Rights Movement and the legal-constitutional language of organized antiwar resistance. Once the petition's argument took hold publicly, the draft could no longer be defended as a neutral instrument of citizenship. It had to be defended, or eventually abandoned, on the terms its critics had set.

From exposure to reform

The eventual transition to an all-volunteer force in 1973 was, in this sense, the federal government's acknowledgment that the system of exemptions could no longer be reconciled with a credible claim to fairness. The exposure of inequality did not just shape public opinion. It made the existing system politically and morally unsustainable, and forced the reform that followed. A draft cannot survive once enough of the public concludes it is rigged, and by the late 1960s that conclusion was no longer in dispute.
