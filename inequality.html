Sub-Claim 2

The Lottery Was Not Random

Social inequality and injustice was prevalent in the draft

The legal structure regarding escape

The Selective Service system was a set of related rules that depended on who one was and what they could afford. More intelligent inductees were usually sent to safer positions like the reserves, while those with less education and fewer resources were sent disproportionately into combat (McCann, "Draft Evasion"). McCann explains the type of draft resistors and how, unfortunately, many were able to be deferred while others couldn’t. It was all dependent on socioeconomic status or education as this allowed for things like channeling or being able to get drafted to a different place. Resistance to the draft or the war itself came in many forms and there were many different ways or extents one would go to to get deferred. Many people’s reaction to the draft was often extreme and led to desertion, fake injuries or familial situations just to not fight the war. However, the unfairness towards members of a lower economic class also was exposed since they couldn’t easily defer. Education-related deferments became the most prominent class divider as a result. A man working full-time at eighteen could not postpone induction into the military unlike a college student.

Thus, the system and society surrounding the draft was biased, which impacted the randomness of the draft itself. In a 1988 Los Angeles Times account, an anonymous draft dodger described that he had paid a lawyer to keep him out of getting drafted, and that he felt no guilt about the individual who had been sent in his place. He argued that the government that had built the system was mostly at fault, and not the men, like him, who were wealthy enough to escape it ("Vietnam War Draft Dodgers," Los Angeles Times). This document captures a single perspective on draft dodging and how rich people could hire lawyers who could get them medical deferments to dodge the draft. This highlights some of the class-based inequalities associated with the draft as rich people could hire lawyers that could get them excused. Additionally, it shows themes of revolution, as many people did this and resisted the draft, showing an entire movement of people refusing to give into the government's needs and focusing on what they wanted to do instead. It demonstrates that movement as it turned in opposition against the government and sought to resist their own way even if the government doesn’t give into their demands.

The 1969 lottery was the official selection process, but statisticians quickly realized that the drawing was not truly random since later months of the year were consistently showing up in the early picks (Caffrey, "Draft Lottery (1969)"). Caffrey provides information occurring within the time period as to how the draft worked and statistics related to it. It described the method for choosing citizens and how statisticians felt about the bias of it. The reactions from statisticians and people today clearly show how flawed the system of the draft lottery was. Additionally, the statistics provided show how big of a revolution this was against the draft draft lottery system.

Despite legal challenges, the courts maintained the lottery results.

The pattern extended beyond the lottery itself. As mentioned earlier, those with more education and higher-up families were more likely to land in relatively calmer positions. Once anti-war groups started to publish the demographics of the frontline and combat ranks, this fact would become more and more obvious.

Calling out injustice

The most direct public question of these inequalities came in a widely popular 1967 antidraft petition. The document argued that since the war was undeclared by Congress, it was therefore unconstitutional. It also said that it violated the moral and religious beliefs of millions of Americans, and that it had drafted African Americans in disproportionate numbers (Accettola, "Civil Rights, Social Justice, and Antiwar Activism"). Accettola shows that organized public resistance against the Vietnam War draft were challenges against the fairness and morality of the draft and that they wanted reform of the system. When they encourage draft refusal, resistance and disobedience, they demonstrate how all of these actions made conscription difficult to enforce, as these movements were organized and well planned, meaning that they were able to make change and show the government that they wanted them to abandon the draft. The document also shows that these problems were typically focused on specific groups and social classes of people, emphasizing the unfairness of the draft, and the reaction they had that pushed them towards reform.

The significance of the petition was the alliance it represented. The criticism of the draft was now paired with the ideologies of the Civil Rights Movement and the organized antiwar resistance efforts. Once the petition's argument entered the public stage, the draft could no longer be defended as neutral.

From exposure to reform

The eventual transition to an all-volunteer force in 1973 was the federal government's acknowledgment and realization that this biased system of deferments could not be supported by the American public anymore. The exposure of inequality did not just shape public opinion, it made the existing system highly disfavored, and forced the reform that followed. The public concluded it was rigged, and by the late 1960s that conclusion was very hard to dispute.
