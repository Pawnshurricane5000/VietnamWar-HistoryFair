Sub-Claim 1

A Draft That Could Not Be Enforced

Civil disobedience and the structural collapse of conscription

As resistance scaled from individual refusal to mass demonstration, the conscription system collided with a population unwilling to comply. Burning draft cards, fleeing the country, and deserting after induction became acts of national consequence, and by the early 1970s the system that had drafted American men since the First World War had become impossible to maintain.

Refusing the call

The first wave of organized resistance came from pacifist organizations that turned individual conscience into coordinated movement. The War Resisters League, founded in 1932, had largely faded during the postwar decades, but the escalation of Vietnam revived its membership from roughly 3,000 in 1964 to 15,000 by 1972 (Tucker, "War Resisters League"). Under leaders David Dellinger and David McReynolds, the WRL cosponsored a 1964 New York City demonstration where draft cards were publicly burned, and helped lead the 1967 mobilization known as Stop the Draft Week. Federal concern was significant enough that the organization was infiltrated by the CIA and targeted by the IRS, a measure of how seriously the state took the movement that was teaching young men how to refuse.

The pacifist core was soon joined by a broader coalition. The antiwar movement of the 1960s drew from earlier 1960s activism and absorbed students, pacifists, writers, and Vietnam veterans into a single front of opposition (DiCanio, "Antiwar Movement"). By 1967, with monthly draft calls exceeding 30,000 men, the movement had outgrown its origins on college campuses and become a national fact. Open protests, marches, and the burning of draft cards became regular features of the public landscape, and the federal government found itself responding less to isolated lawbreakers than to a coherent political movement with leaders, publications, and a strategy.

The price of protest

That public visibility eventually drew an equally public response. The most violent example came on May 4, 1970, at Kent State University, where National Guardsmen opened fire on student demonstrators, killing four. The shootings did not silence the movement; they confirmed it. Public outrage was immediate and lasting, and the moral case for continued conscription, already strained, collapsed entirely. By 1967, more than half of Americans already believed the war was a mistake, and by 1970 a majority wanted troops withdrawn ("Opposition to the Vietnam War," American History).

What Kent State revealed was not simply that the state was willing to use lethal force against its own students. It was that the state could no longer rely on the legitimacy that compulsory service required. Conscription is, at bottom, a moral claim: the nation may take a citizen's body for war because the cause is just and the burden is shared. Once a portion of the public had concluded that the war was not just and the burden was not shared, every additional confrontation, peaceful or violent, only deepened the case against the draft. The deaths at Kent State turned a slogan into a national wound.

The arithmetic of failure

Beneath these public confrontations was a quieter, larger refusal. The Congressional Digest's 1974 official count recorded 495,689 military deserters during the Vietnam era, with an estimated 2,705 fleeing the country entirely. Other estimates place the total of Americans who evaded the draft in some form at more than one million, many of whom slipped across the Canadian border, and only about 200,000 of these were ever formally charged (Caffrey, "Draft Lottery (1969)"; "Vietnam War Draft Evaders & Deserters: An Official Count").

A military that loses nearly half a million men to desertion is not a military that can be reformed through ordinary policy. It is a military whose recruiting model has structurally failed. The Selective Service could continue to send out induction notices, but it could no longer guarantee they would be obeyed. The 1969 lottery, designed to restore the appearance of fairness, only deepened public suspicion when statisticians demonstrated it was not truly random. By the time the Nixon administration began the formal transition away from conscription, the federal government was simply codifying what the public had already done: it had stopped supplying soldiers to the draft.

Why enforcement collapsed

Civil disobedience, then, was not merely a moral protest. It was the operational cause of the all-volunteer force. Every refusing draftee, every fleeing evader, every burned card was a small piece of evidence that the government's authority to compel service had eroded past the point of recovery. The reform that came in 1973 was the federal acknowledgment of a refusal that had been gathering for nearly a decade. Conscription did not end because political leaders chose to end it. It ended because the public chose to stop complying with it, and because no machinery of enforcement could keep pace with the scale of that refusal.
