Section I

Historical Context

The origin of the Vietnam War and the draft system

The Vietnam Conflict until Gulf of Tonkin

Vietnam has been in a state of conflict with many countries attempting to take control. This took a major turn when communist and national leader Ho Chi Minh decided Vietnam was to get its own independence. He defeated French forces in the battle of Dien Bien Phu in 1954 which set the stage for the Geneva Accords to establish a Communist north and southern Republic under Emperor Bao Dai.

With Cold War tensions rising and an effort to maintain a containment policy, a newly communist vietnam threatened American interests. The Gulf of Tonkin incident in 1964 became the official cause for American military escalation, and not so long after the nation was at war.

The lottery

The Selective Service lottery was created based on birth date priority. It involved small capsules which represented every day of the year. These capsules were drawn from a jar at random and whichever date came up first was assigned the highest deployment priority. This was found to be bias towards later birth months.

College enrollment, certain occupations, status related to hardships, and even medical conditions were reasons to be deferred. Those without access to lawyers, doctors, or universities had no such options which was problematic, especially for minority communities.

Photograph of the December 1, 1969 draft lottery drawing, showing the blue plastic capsules being drawn by military officials.

Why this context matters

The draft was built based on a Cold War foreign policy that required compulsory service. This was paired with Selective Service for sending American men to war since World War I. What made Vietnam different was not the existence of the draft but rather the combination of an undeclared war, a clearly unequal lottery, and a generation of New Left individuals.

The Vietnam War draft is one of the most consequential reforms in modern American history. It ended one system, compulsory military service, and produced another, the all-volunteer force, that has defined every American war since. But the story behind that reform is not a story of orderly policymaking. It is a story of refusal. In fact, there are many historical changes that made the most significant impact on the country. One of the most significant is the reform of the Vietnam War draft, which led to a complete transformation of conscription into an all-volunteer army system. However, the path leading to that change resulted in violence due to the growing discontent the citizens of America displayed. During the period from 1960 to 1973, about 2.5 million men were drafted. Hundreds of thousands deserted and more than a million people evaded the draft one way or another. A system that worked for decades during World War I and World War II failed during that decade. This happened because the American society decided not to cooperate any longer with the class inequality the draft demonstrated as well as the amount of innocent lives that were being taken away for a war that did not concern the US government.
